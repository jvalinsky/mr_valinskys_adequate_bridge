## Design Context

### Users
- **Bridge Admin UI**: Developer-operators triaging bridge health, publish/defer/failure queues, ATProto indexer state, reverse-sync mappings, and Room2 operations.
- **SSB Client UI**: Local SSB power users/operators browsing feed/thread data, managing peers and replication, handling blobs, and sending public/private messages.
- **Operational context**: Most sessions are desktop-first and task-driven; users need fast scanning, safe controls, and confidence under failure conditions.

### Brand Personality
- **Pragmatic, curious, humane.**
- **Trustworthy and technical** first, with carefully placed moments of warmth.
- Should feel like a serious operator tool with personality, not a generic enterprise admin shell.
- Anti-reference: interchangeable corporate dashboards or novelty-heavy "hacker" skins.

### Aesthetic Direction
- **Theme model**: Light + dark with explicit user toggle and system fallback.
- **Visual tone**: High-information, scan-friendly technical UI with warm analog green/earth neutrals.
- **Interaction tone**: Clear hierarchy and progressive disclosure; detail-on-demand over visual noise.
- **Technical constraints**: Server-rendered Go templates and embedded CSS/JS (no heavy frontend framework assumptions).

### Design Principles
1. **Operator-first clarity**: Optimize for triage speed, error-state legibility, and safe mutation flows.
2. **Dense but navigable information**: Present critical status immediately, with layered detail for deeper inspection.
3. **Consistency across surfaces**: Keep bridge admin, room admin, and ssb-client interaction language aligned.
4. **Personality through restraint**: Add warmth through tone, color, and micro-interactions without reducing trust.
5. **Accessible by default**: Preserve strong focus states, readable contrast in both themes, keyboard-first paths, and reduced-motion-friendly behavior.
6. **SSR-native implementation**: Prefer robust, maintainable styling patterns compatible with Go template rendering and minimal client script.
